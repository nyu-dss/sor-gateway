Sandy Ground

In the 1840s, the state of Maryland enacted legislation that limited the ability of free Black oystermen to work. Rather than submit to these restrictive laws, several families chose to relocate from the Chesapeake Bay area to another spot well-suited to oyster fishing: Staten Island.  

Originally called Harrisville, Sandy Ground had been home to several free Black families since the abolition of slavery in New York State in 1933, but it was not until this northern migration that it began to expand. At its peak, approximately 150 Black families lived and worked in the Sandy Ground community. Local historians cite it as the “oldest continuously inhabited free Black settlement in the United States.”

However, several environmental factors would lead to a decline in the oyster population around Staten Island and a downturn for the oystering community at Sandy Ground. Overharvesting was a central concern, as the dwindling oyster population was increasingly unable to replenish itself to necessary levels. Environment degradation was another major factor. Raw sewage and industrial waste had been regularly dumped into New York Harbor since the 1840s, and by the early 1900s the water was polluted and the sealife decimated. As a result of this overfishing and environmental destruction, the Sandy Ground oystermen were soon forced to find other occupations, or leave Staten Island completely. 

As William Kornblum points out in his interview with Dr. Roscoe Brown, the degradation of the environment forced Black oystermen out of their work and severed them from the tradition of oystering that formed the basis of their community. Central to Gateway’s plans was a desire to resupply urban residents with access to natural resources and activities that had once been commonplace, while also preserving the natural landscape and protecting it from degradation.
