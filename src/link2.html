Riis Beach

Gateway represented the first time that urban communities in New York, New Jersey, and surrounding states had access to a national park; but it was not the first time that they had access to park land, either in the general vicinity of the city or in what would soon become Gateway itself. 

Jacob Riis Park, which included the extremely popular Riis Beach, was ceded to the National Park Service in 1974. Well before that, the beach was known as a popular gathering site for LGBTQ New Yorkers to gather, often topless or nude. By the 1960s, racially diverse crowds were making enthusiastic use of the beach, drawn by its relative ease of access by public transport and the promise that it was a space where they could truly be themselves.

For any community, access to public space is a vital amenity that allows for gathering and connection. For urban communities, access to green space and other natural environments provides an added escape from dense metropolitan crowds. And for New York’s LGBTQ population in particular, Riis Beach provided a way to create community amongst individuals who could not do so in the private sphere. LGBTQ New Yorkers used Riis Beach as a gathering place to overcome the isolation they may have experienced at home and to form relationships with people like them.

As the plans for Gateway were developed, the example of Riis Beach was both illustrative and challenging. On the one hand, it represented a natural space in which neighbors from all walks of life could — and did — gather. It was successful in drawing visitors, and those visitors were able to use the space in accordance with their desires and needs, thanks to the construction of amenities, including a bathhouse and an ample parking lot

On the other hand, Riis required fairly extensive renovation, having fallen into a state of disrepair due to weather and vandalism, which meant that it was an expensive task to restore the site to its former glory. Gateway staff also had to make tough decisions about what rules to establish and enforce, and ended up enacting a firm policy regarding toplessness and nudity, which had been staples of certain areas of the beach for decades. Even more challenging was figuring out how to extend this kind of local visitorship towards other areas of the park, which often lacked the relative ease of access of Riis Beach and the established amenities and programs to draw in crowds.  

Riis Beach proved to be an interesting microcosm of Gateway as a whole, showcasing both the promise of a public parkland and its accompanying challenges. Would the immediate and long-term planning processes prove successful?
