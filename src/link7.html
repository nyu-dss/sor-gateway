Conservation Corps

The Youth Conservation Corps (YCC) program through the National Park Service (NPS) was designed to introduce young people to the NPS by recruiting them to meaningful paid seasonal work with a strong educational component. This program followed the model first established during the 1930s, when President Franklin Delano Roosevelt’s New Deal legislation created the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC). 

Designed as a public work relief program, the CCC allowed almost three million men to be employed in conserving and developing natural resources, including national parklands and recreation areas. The CCC was a valuable opportunity for men not only to receive food, board, and a consistent paycheck but also to undergo job training and form community with one another. 

Admittedly, there were also problems with the CCC, in particular relating to the under-enrollment and segregation of African-American men, who were among those hit hardest by the Great Depression. The CCC’s founding legislation stated that “no discrimination shall be made on account of race, color or creed.” Despite this, enrollees were frequently sorted into white and Black crews who performed their tasks separately, answered to white supervisors, and slept in segregated campsites. 

CCC enrollees of all kinds contributed an enormous amount to the National Park System in the form of conservation and preservation work, reforestation, construction, and even research and guide services. Nevertheless, for Black enrollees, the legacy of this program was further complicated by exclusionary policies in the parks themselves. For instance, one 1952 survey of 180 state parks found that only 12 were open to Black visitors — even for those who helped to construct them.

Gateway’s planning documents expressed a specific desire to “make Gateway a great park for all the people where innovation and experimentation in park and recreation management, design, and programming may tap the large reservoir of talent, including volunteers, in the region.” The YCC program served as one way to include the expertise and passion of local residents, regardless of age or race, in the work of Gateway.
