Gateway's Planning Process

The planning process for Gateway National Recreation Area was a complex one; it lasted nearly four years and included over 500 informal meetings and interviews as team members sought to balance maintaining the condition and use of the new holdings with evaluating the best ways to develop the park.

The first step was to solicit public involvement through an extended outreach effort, which consisted of several phases. The first, of which these Soul of Reason episodes were a part, involved raising popular awareness about Gateway, contacting community opinion leaders, and launching a telephone and mail campaign to solicit involvement in the product. From there, Gateway staff hosted a series of public workshops, collected ideas for specific plans and then, in phase two, collected alternatives and objections to their work thus far. 

The efficacy of this outreach program was somewhat mixed. The public workshop portion drew around 400 attendees, spread out over 6 events, a lesser total than what the Gateway team had hoped for. And yet, staff members were able to amass a broad set of opinions through a mail-back campaign and hotline, which were collected in the 1977 publication “Here's What We Heard.” 

The community members who participated in these outreach initiatives expressed a desire for environmental preservation, large-scale recreational developments, and facilities and programs that would meet local recreational needs and encourage local use. 

Incorporating this feedback into the General Management Plan was a priority for the Gateway team, and the resultant document sought to “reflect both the emerging public sentiment and the National Park Service's best judgment in resolving the many complex and controversial issues that have surfaced during” the outreach process.

Contingent on Congressional approval, the General Management Plan proposed spending $300 million dollars, in stages. The first stage of the process would aim to minimize disruption to park-goers and neighboring communities while also developing facilities and improving access, while the second and third stages would deal with expanding park capacity and performing large-scale infrastructure projects.
