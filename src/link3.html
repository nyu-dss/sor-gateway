Park Design

Public spaces, no matter their size or provenance, do not emerge fully-formed or by chance. Instead, they are preserved, designed, or protected, in accordance with the complex factors that inform the decision-making process of their planners. These factors, which might include, community stakeholders and user groups, funding sources, and even the egos of the planning team, indelibly shape the project and the final results of the work.

Take New York City’s Central Park as an example. The city’s elites first proposed the idea of the park in the 1840s although the park was not completed until 1876. Among the reasons for the proposal was a hope of enhancing the value of the property they owned in Manhattan, and a desire to create a designated and protected leisure area for white, upper-class women and children. As a result, the years in between the park’s proposal and completion were full of contestation about what exactly the park would be, with various groups within the city arguing for their cause. Who should be able to use the park? For whom should the park be easily accessible? Where should the park be situated? What features should it include? How should it be managed and organized?

The process of considering these questions led to the creation of the Central Park you may be familiar with today, but also left many New Yorkers resentful, alienated, or displaced. 

Original plans for the park selected Jones’s Wood, a 160-acre plot of land on the Upper East Side, as a potential spot for the park; however, difficulties in attaining the land led to the ultimate choice of the 750-acre tract in the center of Manhattan instead. This tract included what was known as Seneca Village, a primarily African-American community. 

Seneca Village was relatively stable and prosperous, with many members of the community owning their own homes, sending their children to school, and working as laborers or servants. These inhabitants were among the New Yorkers displaced by eminent domain throughout the process of acquiring the necessary land to create Central Park. This displacement significantly disrupted the community, as its members were forced to relocate to other neighborhoods within the city or even outside of it. Seneca Village is a striking example of the way that the planning and developing a park can be fraught, not only for those involved but also for those who may be collateral damage in the process of acquisition and development.

In addition to this displacement, poor and working-class New Yorkers, especially Black New Yorkers, had virtually no involvement in the planning process for the park, which left them further alienated from the grand project that many people today conceptualize as egalitarian and public-facing. Early plans focused on elite white activities, with little attention paid to how Black residents might like to make use of the space. In fact, in the 1850’s, Black residents could not even work in construction at the park. 

Despite this exclusion and alienation, wealthy white elites could not stop residents of all kinds from exercising their right to enjoy the newly-opened park, which became a retreat from the stresses of the city and a place to gather and enjoy the outdoors. Just over one hundred years later, Gateway would seek to become a similar retreat for urban populations to enjoy the great outdoors through access to a federal recreation area.
