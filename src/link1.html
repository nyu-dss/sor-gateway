National Park Service

Founded in 1916, the National Park Service (NPS) was established with a central goal: to “conserve the scenery and the natural and historic objects and the wild life [in the national park system] and to provide for the enjoyment of the same in such manner and by such means as will leave them unimpaired for the enjoyment of future generations.” Although parklands, monuments, and battlefields already existed, the NPS’s founding legislation marked the first formal effort to preserve these spaces and places on a federal level, and made explicit the goal of keeping them safe for future users to enjoy.

In the years between its formation and the establishment of Gateway National Recreation Area, the NPS experienced various periods of transition and change, often shaped by historical factors like administrative reorganization, the Great Depression, and new understandings of preservation and environmentalism. By the 1970s, the NPS was experiencing an invigorated focus on ecological conservation, as well as a new directive to bring the resources of the park system to audiences who had lacked access in previous decades.

Gateway National Recreation Area was born from this move towards access. In 1975, when these Soul of Reason episodes were aired, the Gateway team was still very much entrenched in the planning process for the park. They had a distinct vision, one that both reinforced and added to the initial goal of the NPS. They hoped that Gateway would “preserve and protect for the use and enjoyment of present and future generations an area possessing outstanding natural and recreational features” — but that’s not all. The General Management Plan, published in 1979, also articulated a more specific commitment to draw on the talent and needs of the diverse surrounding urban communities to create a strong relationship between them and the natural environment available at Gateway. 

Gateway National Recreation Area was conceptualized during a period of expansion and redefinition for the National Park Service, a period where the NPS was moving beyond simply preserving significant natural resources and meaningful historic sites in order to actively engage new audiences in these spaces. Gateway’s explicit commitment to diversity and access is thus reflective of the new priorities of the NPS in the period.
